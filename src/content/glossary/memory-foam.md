# What is Memory Foam

**Memory foam is a viscoelastic polyurethane foam that softens in response to heat and pressure, conforming closely to the body's shape before slowly returning to its original form.**

Memory foam, originally developed by NASA in the 1960s for seat cushioning to improve crash protection, is engineered for its unique density and slow-rebound properties. Its "viscoelastic" nature means it is both viscous (allowing it to flow and conform) and elastic (enabling it to bounce back). The foam reacts to body heat, allowing it to mold precisely to contours, and its open-cell structure is designed to promote airflow, though traditional memory foam is often noted for retaining heat. Advanced versions now incorporate gel infusions, copper, or graphite to improve breathability and cooling.

This material matters most in the sleep industry for its ability to provide exceptional pressure relief. By evenly distributing weight, it reduces pressure points at the shoulders, hips, and heels, which can significantly improve comfort and alignment for side sleepers and those with joint pain. Its contouring nature also minimizes motion transfer, meaning movement on one side of the bed is less likely to disturb a partner—a key benefit for couples. For example, a memory foam mattress can cradle a person's curved spine in side-sleeping position, while a traditional spring mattress might leave gaps that create uncomfortable pressure.

Beyond mattresses, memory foam's principles are applied in mattress toppers (for a cost-effective comfort upgrade), pillows (for neck support), and even shoe insoles. When considering memory foam, key related factors are **density** (weight per cubic foot, indicating durability and support), **ILD** (Indentation Load Deflection, measuring firmness), and the presence of **infusions** like gel for cooling. It is also a primary component in **all-foam** or **hybrid mattresses**, the latter combining foam with coil systems for added support and breathability.
